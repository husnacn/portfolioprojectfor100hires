**Content**

I was raised thinking that Money was something you worked for, not something that worked for you.

My grandfather immigrated to France with empty pockets.

My parents never went to university.

So when I grew lemlist from 0 to a $150M valuation and made a life changing amount of money, I discovered a completely different problem.

Building a company is hard. But the rules are legible.

Work hard. Build something people want. Don't run out of money.

Wealth management has no legible rules.

It has a language designed to confuse you, a cast of characters whose incentives are misaligned with yours, and a long history of extracting money from people who don't know what they don't know.

The first time my private bank called me after I had money, they arrived with products.

Complex ones. Structured notes. Multi-layered funds….

Things that took hours and multiple spreadsheet to understand.

That complexity wasn't sophistication.

It was camouflage.

Because the financial industry doesn't make money when you make money.

It makes money when you transact. When you trust. When you don't ask questions.

The moment you understand this, every interaction changes.

First-generation wealth has one advantage old money doesn't.

No assumptions.

Old money trusts the private banker because their father trusted the private banker.

They sit in the same offices, speak the same language, attend the same dinners.

I had none of that. No relationships to inherit. No trust pre-installed.

So I made mistakes... Trusted blindly. Paid more fees that I should…

Now, I follow a simple rule, I walk away from every structure I can’t explain to myself in plain language.

The game isn't complicated once you see it clearly.

Own things that compound.

Invest only with people who also invest their own money.

Never trust anyone whose fee goes up when your complexity goes up.

## Post 5
**Date:** April 2, 2026

**URL:** [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/guillaume-moubeche-a026541b2_the-private-market-industry-has-a-dirty-secret-activity-7445354589048184832-faUy?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAABvCC-8BaIYJGZPLSBP-LrMAx45vUNX38eE]

**Content**

The private market industry has a dirty secret 👇

The less you understand what you're buying, the more money someone else is making.

You want to invest in a great private company. 

You find someone who can get you in. 

They put you inside a structure (SPV) with a few fees. 

Nothing crazy on its own.

2% to enter. 1% per year. 10% of the profits at the end.

Fine. You accept.

What you don't realize is that this vehicle is part of another vehicle.

The guy who sold it to you bought it from someone else. Who bought it from someone else.

Each one added their own 2%. Their own 1%. Their own 20%.

By the time the money reaches the actual company, you're actually layer 3…

The company does well. You made money on paper.

Then you do the math on what you actually received.

It's not even 20% of what you expected.

5 years ago, I was that person.

Invested in a late stage company that did 20x on paper.

Got a 3x return.

Not because the bet was wrong.

Because people got paid (a lot more) before I did.

The rule I use now is embarrassingly simple.

If I can't explain exactly what I'm paying (and to who) in 20 seconds flat, I don't invest.

Good investments don't need to hide behind complexity.

Only the fees do.

## Post 8
**Date:** March 19, 2026

**URL:** [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/guillaume-moubeche-a026541b2_i-made-30-early-stage-investments-as-an-activity-7439969465598926848-hqSs?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAABvCC-8BaIYJGZPLSBP-LrMAx45vUNX38eE]

**Content**

I made 30+ early stage investments as an angel. Here's what nobody explained to me.

When you write a check for a seed round, you feel like an investor.

You're not. You're last in line.

Every round after yours brings new investors with better preferences.

Series A gets 1x liquidation preference. Series B gets another 1x. Series C gets another 1x… and so on and so forth...

By the time there's an exit, there's a queue and you're at the back of it.

I've seen founders sell for $100M and their earliest believers walk away with almost nothing.

Not a bad outcome at all. Just a brutal cap table.

Late stage secondaries are a different game entirely.

You buy at the latest preference stack.

You're not getting crushed by subsequent rounds because you're already at the top (or close to the top).

The company works. Revenue is real.

You're not funding the dream. You're buying the proof.

Early stage is a portfolio game. You need 30+ bets to find a couple of real winner.

Late stage private tech is a conviction game.

Five good positions can change your portfolio permanently.

Revolut is currently valued at $100B+. 

Here’s why I think it could become a trillion dollar company:

When I look at any late stage company I ask three questions.

1- Is the problem they're solving permanent?

2- Do they get stronger as they grow?

3- Are incumbents structurally unable to compete?

Revolut answers yes to all three.

1- The problem is permanent.

My kids will never walk into a bank.

They will never spend 45 minutes on hold to authorize a wire transfer.

Never fill out a form in triplicate to open an account in another country.

Never pay 3% in foreign exchange fees because a building on a high street needs to exist.

Traditional banking wasn't designed for the world we live in.

It was designed for a world where information moved slowly, borders mattered operationally, and switching costs were so high nobody bothered.

That world is gone.

Revolut now has 65 million customers and will hit $9B+ in revenue by the end of the year.

This isn't a startup story anymore. It's a compounding machine.

2- They get stronger as they grow.

Every new user makes the payment network more useful.

Every new product (crypto, stock trading, business accounts, mortgages etc..) increases switching costs.

WeChat didn't win China because it had the best product.
It won because leaving meant losing access to everyone you knew.

Revolut is building that same gravitational pull globally.

3- Incumbents can't catch up. Structurally.

I've used the “best” taditional european banks for years.

The gap between what they offer and what Revolut offers isn't a product gap. It's an architecture gap.

Traditional banks run on systems built in the 1970s. Every new feature requires a compliance committee, a vendor contract, a 18-month integration project, and a risk committee that says no.

Revolut ships products in weeks.

You cannot fix this with budget.

You cannot fix this with consultants.

The org structure, the incentive system, the regulatory posture… all of it was built for a different era.

The only way a traditional bank competes with Revolut is to become Revolut. 

And they won't. 

Because the moment they do, they cannibalize everything they've built.

**Content**

Most VCs have never run a company.

Anish Acharya has built two, sold both (one to Google + one to Credit Karma), and then joined Andreessen Horowitz as General Partner.

I sat down with him for BILLIONS and left with 5 ideas I can't stop thinking about 👇

1/ There are no distribution problems. Only product problems.

When you're struggling to grow, the temptation is to hire more salespeople or spend more on ads. 
Anish's take: if distribution is hard, your product isn't good enough yet. 
--> Great products pull people in they don't need to be pushed.

2/ Paternalism kills products every time.

Every product that tells users what they *should* do fails. 
The ones that work with human nature (not against it) win. 
Cultural tailwinds are free leverage. Fighting them is just expensive.

3/ AI creates jobs. It doesn't kill them.

AI handles the 80% that's repetitive. The 20% left — judgment, relationships, navigating ambiguity — that's what humans are actually for. The people who lose jobs are the ones who refuse to use AI. The ones who gain? The ones who use it to do things that weren't possible before.

4/ Winning is underrated for life satisfaction.

Not just financially. 
The sustained feeling of contributing to something that actually works; that's the thing people don't talk about enough. 
Find the thing you can contribute most to. Then go win at it.

5/ Consumers will now pay 15X more for software.

Pricing went from $20/month to $300/month almost overnight for AI products. That's not inflation, that's a fundamental shift in how people value software. 
It also means you no longer need ads to build a massive business. 
Direct monetization is back.

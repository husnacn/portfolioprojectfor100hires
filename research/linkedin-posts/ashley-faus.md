How high shall we sing for mic check... SOUND ON for this one! 🎶

I opened "Sister Act" this weekend, and because I'm a musical theater nerd and a marketing nerd, I do, in fact, have a bunch o' lessons to share at the intersection of these two passions 😀

Lesson 1: Actors Don't Give Notes to Other Actors

Imagine you've got 20+ people onstage, each trying to do their own part to make the show amazing... and after every scene or musical number, a subset of those people start telling others that they need to move a little to stage right, they were too slow on their turns, they dropped a line, might they try this gesture instead?

It would be quite confusing.

For some reason, we allow creation-by-committee in marketing, and we're confused when it causes a whole lotta friction.

Take a lesson from the theater: notes come from the director, not other actors.

Lesson 2: Call and Response Rituals

"Places for Act 1!"
"Thank you places!"

"You are dismissed!"
"Thank you dismissed!"

We are conditioned to respond to a specific set of updates from our stage manager. 

The entire cast is meant to respond when they make the call, and it serves two purposes: first, it ensures YOU know what's happening. Second, it ensures that anyone who wasn't within earshot of the announcement hears what's happening, since multiple people loudly repeat the same information.

When it's time to launch a product, where do you share the plan? One measly Slack channel? 

When it's time to share the retro, where do you share the information? One little internal blog post?

Take a note from the theater and institute call-and-response rituals to make sure EVERYONE is on the same page.

Lesson 3: Planning, Planning, Planning

I have multiple quick costume changes in this show, some that involve removing 4+ costume pieces and donning 6+ costume pieces, including a complicated nun's habit.

The order of operations matters A LOT for making it onstage after a quick-change!

For this reason, I'm careful to set up all my costume pieces in the perfect order, so that when I sprint backstage, I can start dressing immediately.

But in business, we love a curveball 🤦‍♀️

We'll put together a run-of-show, a list of reviewers, a whole campaign launch workback plan... and then just let a few more people weigh in at the 11th-hour, add just one more asset the day before go-live, or attempt to swap speakers a week before the event.

And then we wonder why we're half-naked at showtime 😳

Take a note from the theater: STICK TO THE PLAN!

The throughline for ALL of these lessons:

✅ Defined roles and responsibilities
✅ Clear communication
✅ Strategic planning

"Everything I want to say has already been said... so why should I share?" 😳

"I'm the ONLY person who has ever said this! It's MY idea and MY process!" 😤

These two statements are two sides of the same coin.

The reality is that it's a big world, and yes, it's hard to have truly original ideas.

This leads some people to never share, because what's the point?

And it leads others to be combative because they insist everyone else is copying them, or people insist that they're plagiarizing.

To mitigate BOTH fears, here's 3 phrases I recommend:

✅ In my opinion...
✅ From my perspective...
✅ In my experience...

These phrases help the audience understand where YOU are coming from, which is unique to YOU.

It also sets up a friendly dynamic for agreement, additional experiences, and more perspectives.

A few of my key takeaways:

🗓️ Build buffer time into timelines so people can actually experiment

Too many teams end up separating the AI adoption process from the actual work process.

In reality, an incremental approach to ensure you're actually solving a real problem in the right way improves the long-term outcomes with AI.

🤝 Run a Fix-it-Friday to solve problems together

Lots of teams have a handful of individuals who are doing amazing things with AI... but that's not translating to improved TEAMwide outcomes.

Bringing everyone together to solve one specific problem balances the individual experimentation with the shared insights and processes.

📊 Choose ONE data set to start

AI outputs are only as good as the inputs used to analyze, generate, and summarize information.

This means that if your tags, infrastructure, and taxonomy are lacking... your AI outputs are gonna struggle.

It's overwhelming to think about cleaning up and optimizing EVERYTHING, so start with one core set of data and expand when you see reliable outcomes.

"We've gotten incredibly good at building. We haven't gotten any better at knowing what deserves to exist." - Vassilena Valchanova

I love the call-out about knowing what deserves to exist, but I'm a bit conflicted 😅

The underlying assumption is that everything we build needs to be sellable and profitable. 

There's little or no space for "wasted" work.

But the ad doesn't say, "Build your business by your next stop" or "Make a profit by your next stop".

It focuses on bringing an idea to life, quickly.

And THAT is a different thing, and one that I think gets lost in the race to win capitalism. 

I think we need a balance of work that's meant to meet a market need in a profitable way, and "work" that's meant to serve our desire to create, experiment, and solve our own quirky problems. 

We don't see hobbies like drawing or pottery or scrapbooking as "not deserving to exist", so I think it's worth separating some digital creation outputs into the same "create because creativity is fun" category.

The joke's on us if we allow AI to force us even further down the all-money-all-the-time path.

## Post 6
**Date:** April 2, 2026
**URL:** [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ashleyfaus_sometimes-adults-need-security-blankets-activity-7444390520157708289-4592?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAABvCC-8BaIYJGZPLSBP-LrMAx45vUNX38eE]
**Content**
Sometimes adults need security blankets, too! 😅

I was advising on a video set for a marketing video recently, and our speaker was getting a little flustered.

She had been heavily involved in shaping the talking points, spent hours prepping herself over the weekend, and walked into the shoot feeling confident.

But when the bright lights turned on and the blinking red light indicated that we were rolling, she froze.

We took a break to let her review her talking points, and she walked over to me, script in-hand, folded in half... and said ALL of her talking points perfectly 🤔

How the heck did THAT happen!?!

I recognized two things:

1) She's comfortable with me, so talking to me feels like a conversation, not a performance.

2) She was holding her script. And that feeling of the paper in hands, just like she practiced, made her feel confident that she could talk, without worrying about going blank. 

I see this over and over in musical theater, where singers and actors walk around holding a nearly-closed book for weeks, simply because it FEELS better.

Putting the book down feels like you're losing your security blanket, and now you're just up there, bare, waiting to drop a line or miss a note 😅

Eventually, it feels silly or restrictive to hold your book, and you can leave it backstage with no qualms.

The thing is, we rarely allow time for this phase in our B2B presentations 🤦‍♀️

We hustle to finish the slides at midnight the night before, and then we hurry to run through 'em a few times before we step onstage the next morning.

But the security blanket phase solidifies the script and the taking points by continuing mind-body connection that helps with memorization and fluency!

So... next time you've got enough time to prep... hold a closed script for a few run-throughs, and see if it helps improve your flow 😀

**URL:** [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ashleyfaus_the-journey-has-never-been-linear-but-apparently-activity-7442584871799238656-p6Ti?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAABvCC-8BaIYJGZPLSBP-LrMAx45vUNX38eE]
**Content**
The journey has NEVER been linear, but apparently we're pretending that AI suddenly made this true 🤦‍♀️

NOPE.

No one wakes up and declares, "I shall enter the Consideration stage today!"

The linear funnel (and it's newer cousin, the looping decision journey) is all about a marketer trying to imply where someone is in a buying process or force them down a specific path with a single destination: BUY TODAY!!!

Heck, even the name, "buyer's journey" assumes this person becomes a buyer, when in fact, 40%-60% of journeys end in no sale/no decision 🤦‍♀️

Pitfalls of the linear funnel:

- Assumes the journey starts at "awareness", when in fact, the journey starts long before someone becomes aware of the problem, solution, your company, and/or your offering

- Generally fails to recognize the difference between a user and an economic buyer, and struggles to acknowledge the complexity of a buying committee

- Focuses on implicit intent vs. explicit intent, with marketing and sales teams attempting to guess at signals of buying readiness amid a sea of noise

Instead, we need to treat the buyer's journey as a playground.

Our job as marketers is to make that playground seamless, helpful, and impactful, no matter which path the audience takes.

## Post 9
**Date:** March 26, 2026
**URL:** [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ashleyfaus_we-should-not-be-celebrating-and-rewarding-activity-7440765971499220993-zNvm?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAABvCC-8BaIYJGZPLSBP-LrMAx45vUNX38eE]
**Content**
🌶️ We should NOT be celebrating and rewarding "going above and beyond" 🌶️

How often have you seen this type of praise:

"She worked long hours and into the night to make this happen..." 

"He spent the entire weekend handling this..." 

"The team pulled off the impossible..."

My question is: What happened to force people to be heroes?

Did you fail to staff the project appropriately? 
Did you impose a ridiculous deadline? 
Did you cut the budget to the bone?

Because when heroism becomes the norm, that's a management failure, not a badge of honor.

Yes, genuine crises happen, sometimes it's all-hands-on-deck for one final push, sometimes things go sideways.

But if your team is constantly being asked to save the day, the problem isn't effort, it's planning and resourcing.

The answer should never be burning out the team, over and over, instead of fixing the system.

Recognize and compensate people when it happens? 100%. 

Celebrate it as a model to repeat? NOPE.

Don't under-resource and over-burden your team...

How I approach this as a manager:

✅️ Build trust with my team to raise capacity concerns by regularly talking about workload and timelines

✅️ Run retros after big projects to document what went well and find areas to improve before we tackle it next time

✅️ Push back on quick-turn requests that don't have a clear driver of urgency or ROI

✅️ Add buffer to timelines when possible to account for cross-functional dependencies or external factors

✅️ Guidance on PTO at regular intervals, with the expectation of down time after a big push for a deadline or event
